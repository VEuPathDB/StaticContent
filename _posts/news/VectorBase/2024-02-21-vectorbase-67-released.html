VEuPathDB named as an Elixir Core Data Resource

Inducted in December 2023, VEuPathDB is honored to join the select group of Elixir Core Data Resources (CDR). With this distinction, VEuPathDB is identified as a resource of fundamental importance to the life science community and the preservation of biological data, dedicated to the principle of open access for publicly funded research. The CDR program recognizes resources that promote excellence and provide infrastructure vital for the advancement of the life sciences. Evaluation criteria include scientific focus, quality of science and community service, governance, impact, and ability to facilitate scientific research.

Genome Sequence and Annotation

The following organisms are represented in VectorBase for the first time.
  • Anopheles bellator AbelBR1 – GCF_943735745.2
  • Anopheles coustani AcouGA1 – GCF_943734705.1
  • Anopheles ziemanni AzieGA1 – GCF_943734765.1

Genome Updates

  • Glossina fuscipes IAEA 2018 – This data set was updated with the 2022 annotation version from the INSDC repository. GCA_014805625.1; Saarman et al. 2022 (preprint)

Genome Reference Changes

  • Anopheles arabiensis DONGOLA 2021 now serves as the VectorBase reference for Phlebotomus species. All functional genomics data will be aligned to this genome. Anopheles arabiensis Dongola remains available on VectorBase, but with limited functionality.
  • Anopheles stephensi UCISS2018 now serves as the VectorBase reference for Anopheles stephensi strains. All functional genomics data will be aligned to this genome. Anopheles stephensi SDA-500 remains available on VectorBase, but with limited functionality.
  • Anopheles merus MAF 2021 – now serves as the VectorBase reference for Anopheles merus strains. All functional genomics data will be alighned to this genome. Anopheles merus MAF remains available on VectorBase, but with limited functionality.

Annotation Updates


RNA Sequence

Aedes aegypti LVP_AGWG
  • Female antennae from six-time points (5-10 days) post-emergence, non-blood fed (nbf) and age-matched blood-fed (bf) cohorts – Female antenna from six-time points, 5 to 10 days post-emergence (dpe), non-blood fed (nbf) and age-matched blood-fed (bf) cohorts (5 to 10 dpe bf), spanning the adult female's first gonotrophic cycle. Hill et al. 2021.
Anopheles arabiensis DONGOLA 2021
  • Evolution of sex-biased gene expression aaraDongola – Male and female sex-tissues vs carcass transcriptomes. 3-4 day old adult males and 48 hour blood-fed females. Reproductive tissues collected from females included the ovaries and the common oviduct and for males included the testis and accessory glands. The remaining carcass (CA) included the head, thorax and abdomen. Papa et al. 2017.
  • Generic RNAseq for gene prediction aaraDongola – Anopheles 15 Genomes RNA portion. Neafsey et al. 2015.
  • Generic RNAseq (part of a study with +8 mosquito species) – Eggs from all 10 species were obtained through the Malaria Research and Reference Reagent Resource Center (http://www.mr4.org/), 36-bp Solexa (one biological replicate). Hittinger et al. 2010.
  • RNA-Seq from male and female Anopheles coluzzii, Anopheles arabiensis, Anopheles quadriannulatus, and their hybrids – RNA-Seq of member species of the Anopheles gambiae species complex and their F1 hybrids Deitz et al. 2018.
  • Evolution of gene expression levels in Anopheles male reproductive organs aaraDongola – Gene expression was determined for Male accessory gland and testes. Izquierdo et al. 2019.
  • A. arabiensis RNA seq Raw sequence reads – Cross-species Y chromosome function between malaria vectors of the Anopheles gambiae species complex. Bernardini et al. 2017.
Anopheles coluzzii AcolN3
  • Virgin male testes and carcass (body without testes) – 5-10-d-old virgin male testes (without accessory glands), pools of 200 mosquito testes or 20 carcasses (four biological replicates), 100 bp paired-end reads. Cassone et al. 2017.
Anopheles merus MAF 2021
  • Virgin male testes and carcass (body without testes) – 5-10-d-old virgin male testes (without accessory glands), pools of 200 mosquito testes or 20 carcasses (four biological replicates), 100 bp paired-end reads. Cassone et al. 2017.
  • Generic RNA-Seq for gene prediction – Neafsey et al. 2015.
Anopheles stephensi UCISS2018
  • Gene trapping – RNA from 200 pooled individually-extracted An. stephensi that demonstrated a gene trap remobilizable event. Reid et al. 2018.
  • Generic RNAseq (part of a study with +8 mosquito species) – Eggs from all 10 species were obtained through the Malaria Research and Reference Reagent Resource Center (http://www.mr4.org/), 36-bp Solexa (one biological replicate)Hittinger et al. 2010.
  • MSQ43 cell line RNA-Seq – Unpublished transcriptome sequencing of Anopheles stephensi MSQ43 cell lines. PRJNA238706
  • Anopheles stephensi transcriptome project – This transcriptome project is being performed at the University of Tokyo, Department of Medical Genome Sciences PRJDA33439.
  • Generic RNA-Seq for gene prediction – Neafsey et al. 2015.
Dermacentor silvarum Dsil-2018
  • Questing/active adults from vegetation, summer diapause, and overwintering from leaf litter – Ticks were collected in three conditions: questing/active adults from vegetation, summer diapause, and overwintering from leaf litter (one biological sample for each). PRJNA565692.
Ixodes scapularis PalLabHiFi
  • Unfed to post-engorgement midguts of adult females – Midguts from adult females were collected at different stages during blood feeding, including unfed ticks. The study included 10 conditions categorized by average weight or hours post-detachment, each with three biological replicates. Lu et al. 2023.
Leptotrombidium deliense UoL-UT
  • L. pallidum larvae and adult female mapped to L. deliense – Four paired-end libraries (larvae, protonymphs, male adults, and female adults) are mentioned in the paper, but only two BioSamples are available from NCBI (with one biological sample from each). PRJNA540739.
Phlebotomus papatasi M1
  • Wild-caught P. kandelakii female salivary glands aligned to P. papatasi – Female salivary glands from wild-caught Phlebotomus kandelakii from Tbilisi, Georgia. Oliveira et al. 2020.
  • Blood (with and without parasites) at 1, 4, or 9 days post blood meal – Female sand flies aged 5 to 9 days were fed blood or parasite-containing blood, resulting in four treatment groups: (i) blood meal only, (ii) blood meal + L. major, (iii) blood meal + L. donovani and (iv) blood meal + H. muscarum. Flies were then left to develop an infection or digest their non-infected blood meal for 1, 4 or 9 days (three biological replicates). Sloan et al. 2021.

Note: Our bimonthly database releases incorporate new data and correct errors in old data when necessary. Changes in annotation and new experimental data may slightly alter your search results by increasing or decreasing the number of hits. When search parameters change with a new release, we invalidate (Ø) the search and ask you to rerun it. When IDs are updated or removed, we map the old IDs to the new ones, remove the old IDs from your Basket, and leave your Favorites page alone.
